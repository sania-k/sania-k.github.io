2025 NSF Grants Termination

September 19, 2025
4 minutes

The National Science Foundation (NSF)1 is one of the leading sources of funding for research in the United States. Each year, this independent federal agency funds projects across science disciplinesfrom Arctic ice studies to teaching methods in math class. For researchers at a variety of institutions across the country, the NSF and similar foundations are essential to support research.

However, earlier this year, NSF had to make big changes. A White House memo temporarily froze new grants and spending across all government organizations, citing concerns over funding programs tied to foreign aid, diversity, and green initiatives 2. The memo was quickly rescinded, but the priorities for future funding were shifted, and with that came the termination of awarded project grants3.

This raises a critical questionwhat kinds of research lost support, and what does that reveal about national priorities in science?

When we map the terminated grants by directorate (NSFs broadest categories for funding), we can see that one category stands out.

Figure 1. Sunburst pie chart distrubution of funds cancelled for projects across NSF directorates. Across all directorates, over $600 million in awards were cancelled, making up over 5% of NSF's 2025 budget request to Congress 4 and less than 0.01% of federal spending in the fiscal year 5. The STEM Education directorate makes up a total of 52.5% of the total funding cancelled. To see more about each directorate, click on them to see the distribution across divisions in more detail.

More than half of all cuts, over $300 million in funding, came from STEM Education. This directorate funds projects and scholarships aimed at making STEM opportunities available to all Americans through scholarships and pedagogical research and everything in between 6. While at first glance this looks like a general cut to education, we can break it down further to see the majority of these canceled projects came from the Division of Research on Learning in Formal and Informal Settings. This division supports programs to improve the teaching and learning of science in preK-12 classrooms, museums, and science centers, all done to make STEM learning accessible to students 7.

The second-largest source of terminations was the Directorate for Social, Behavioral and Economic Sciences. They support research on human behavior, social organization, and how cultural and political forces shape society. In short, it funds work on how people and communities function7.

Beyond these two, the remaining cuts were relatively small and spread across the sciences. The only exception is the smallest category, the Office of the Director, which is usually focused on administrative oversight rather than research8.

Through all of this, the data suggests that these cuts didnt just uniformly cut budgets, but that they disproportionately affected projects focused on equity and accessibility in science education, therefore limiting future career opportunities for many.  

This story becomes clearer when we compare the words used in cancelled grant titles.

Figure 2. Treemap of the most common words found in grant project titles, categorized by directorate. Click into directoriates to see common words in more detail.

Common terms such as collaborative and research were filtered out. What remains now are predominantly words tied to equity and inclusionterms like equity, community, racial, culture, diversity, and inclusivity.

Across all the directorates, projects on improving accessibility or addressing social inequality have been shown as those that are most likely to be terminated. 

Finally, we look into the project abstracts. As the most detailed part of the data set, will they provide the most insight into the increasingly apparent trends?

Bubble chart of the most commonly repeated phrases found in terminated project abstracts. Hover over bubbles to see the exact count

The bubble chart of abstracts shows us similar results to what we saw the titles. While the most commonly repeated phrases are about their project missions, on NSF ideals, about advancing knowledge, and broadening impact of their research, if we look further down the list of frequenct phrases we start seeing the same trend. Phrases like "stem education", "racial equity", "stem learning", "broadening participation" and "equity inclusion" all indicate that a considerable amount of these projects are related to accessiblity. So although the abstracts are more focused on the minutiae of their project, we can all see that many of these projects dealt with accessibilty, further supported from the outer figures.

From here the pattern is apparent, projects terminated are clustered around areas like STEM education, accessibility in scientific research and careers, and social science. These were all projects accepted by NSF as having enough value to fund with their relatively limited bugdet. These are investments into how science is taught, who gets access to these (often higher paying) careers, and how society understands itselfall of it has been limited. With programs like these being cut, another question remains.

What kind of future are we choosing to fund, and who benefits from it?
